US Commerce Department issues first-ever AI model export ban — Claude Fable 5 and Mythos pulled globally

The US Department of Commerce issued an export-control directive on 12 June 2026 ordering Anthropic to block all foreign nationals — including its own non-US employees — from accessing the newly released Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, citing national security concerns over a jailbreak that had allegedly been exploited by a Chinese threat group. Unable to comply while keeping the models selectively accessible, Anthropic disabled them for all users worldwide. The directive marks the first time a government has applied export controls directly to an AI model rather than to chips or hardware. Anthropic publicly contested the rationale, calling the jailbreak "narrow" and warning that applying this standard across the industry "would essentially halt all new model deployments for all frontier model providers." As of 15 June, Anthropic was in active talks with the Trump administration over the Mythos dispute.

Why it matters: Extending export controls from hardware to model weights sets a significant precedent for how governments may assert sovereign control over frontier AI capabilities. Any organisation operating across jurisdictions — and especially those deploying AI APIs where end-users span nationalities — now has a concrete illustration of how abruptly model access can be revoked for non-technical reasons. The event also exposes a new dimension of AI supply-chain risk: enterprise architectures that rely on a single frontier model provider without fallback options carry concentration risk that this episode has made operationally visible.

EU AI Office publishes final Code of Practice for marking AI-generated content — August 2 deadline unchanged

The European AI Office published the final Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content on 10 June 2026, providing practical implementation guidance to help generative AI providers and deployers meet Article 50 transparency obligations that become enforceable on 2 August 2026. The Code specifies two required technical mechanisms: digitally-signed metadata and imperceptible watermarking for AI-generated or AI-manipulated content published on matters of public interest. Deepfakes must be clearly labelled; users must be notified when interacting with AI chatbots. The guidance is voluntary in design but maps directly to what compliance with the enforceable Article 50 requires.

Why it matters: With fewer than eight weeks to the August 2 enforcement date, this Code is the clearest signal yet of what Article 50 compliance will look like in practice. Any organisation producing synthetic text, images, audio, or video for public-facing channels in the EU — or deploying AI chatbots in customer interactions — needs to assess whether its current disclosure and watermarking approach meets these standards before the deadline. The metadata and watermarking norms being set here are also likely to influence comparable guidance in other jurisdictions given the EU's standard-setting track record.
