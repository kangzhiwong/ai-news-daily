AI Governance & Regulation

European Parliament adopts AI Act Digital Omnibus — high-risk deadline pushed 16 months to December 2027, new nudification ban added

The European Parliament voted 423 in favour on 16 June 2026 to adopt the Digital Omnibus on Artificial Intelligence, a package that rewrites the AI Act's application timeline and makes targeted simplifications. The most immediate change: obligations for high-risk AI systems (Annex III) are pushed from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027 — a 16-month extension. High-risk AI embedded in regulated products (Annex I) shifts from August 2027 to August 2028. Alongside the deadline relief, the package introduces a new ban on AI-generated non-consensual intimate images ("nudification"), expands the SME simplified-compliance threshold to companies with up to 750 employees and €150M revenue, and relaxes AI literacy obligations from "ensure" to "take measures to support." The EU Council is expected to formally adopt the text on 29 June 2026.

Why it matters: The 16-month extension on high-risk AI compliance significantly changes the planning horizon for any organisation deploying AI in consequential decision-making. Teams that mobilised compliance programmes for the August 2026 deadline need to reassess resource allocation — but the extension should not be read as the rules softening. The new nudification ban and the standing enforcement infrastructure (60-member Scientific Panel, Advisory Forum) signal that substantive protections are hardening even as operational deadlines shift. Organisations in scope for the EU AI Act should now replan timelines against December 2027 while maintaining momentum on foundational work.

EU AI Office publishes final Code of Practice for marking AI-generated content — August 2 deadline unchanged

The European AI Office published the final Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content on 10 June 2026, providing practical implementation guidance to help generative AI providers and deployers meet Article 50 transparency obligations that become enforceable on 2 August 2026. The Code specifies two required technical mechanisms: digitally-signed metadata and imperceptible watermarking for AI-generated or AI-manipulated content published on matters of public interest. Deepfakes must be clearly labelled; users must be notified when interacting with AI chatbots. The guidance is voluntary in design but maps directly to what compliance with the enforceable Article 50 requires.

Why it matters: With fewer than eight weeks to the August 2 enforcement date, this Code is the clearest signal yet of what Article 50 compliance will look like in practice. Any organisation producing synthetic text, images, audio, or video for public-facing channels in the EU — or deploying AI chatbots in customer interactions — needs to assess whether its current disclosure and watermarking approach meets these standards before the deadline. The metadata and watermarking norms being set here are also likely to influence comparable guidance in other jurisdictions given the EU's standard-setting track record.
